Development Style

There are generally two approaches to development, which fairly contrast each other. Imperative style describe how to do things in orderly fashion. Declarative style describes what needs to be done. Low-level style is called procedural development style, and high-level style is called object-oriented paradigm. Procedural style is essentially constrained with structures and procedures (hence the name). Procedure is just another term used for function. Object-Oriented paradigm is centered around objects. Objects couple structures and functions operating on them together. This bundle is called an object. I will discuss both of those styles in more detail. About Tools

Development presents us with a significant tradeoff which deeply affects both our tools and nature of our code. That is a tradeoff between a speed of development and speed of execution. Fastest programs are the closest to the machine, and most convenient programs require a number of abstractions, indirections and execution overhead. The most known low-level language (besides assembly, which is just a thin wrapper) is C. There are some more new languages in this field, like Rust or Zig. I do not mention C++, because it accomplishes an amazing achievement of being both human-unreadable and machine-unreadable. Most people enter the world of development from high-level dynamically-typed languages, that take care of almost anything you could think of. It makes those languages, like Python or JavaScript, very easy to learn. One of tradeoffs here is that those languages have poor execution speed due to the fact that they provide a lot of convenience like dynamic typing and automatic memory management. Another drawback is that those languages often scale poorly due to their dynamic typing nature, because it can be hard to work with data when you are not sure about it’s type.
