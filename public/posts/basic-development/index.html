Basic Development

August 17, 2024

So, you want to become a developer? It surely is a fine aspiration. But before we proceed, let me ask you a question. Why do you want to become a developer? Do you like computers? Are you intrigued by how they work? Do you wish to command computers in the same manner as sorcerer commands mysterious spirits? Do you want to unfold this world of magic and jump into that rabbit hole? Was your answer somewhere here? If not, I’d like you to pause for a second and think. I am deeply convinced that anyone can do anything. I am not saying that it’s going to be easy, but it’s certainly possible. Whatever you want to do, as long as you want to do it, and as long as you apply your effort, you can succeed. Again, there is, of course, a place for a talent. But I think of “talent” as a function of determination and experience. So, let me ask you again: why do you want to become a developer? Of course, there are money involved, and it was a fairly popular career not long time ago. But if you are from 2024, I’m sure that you see what is going on. Job market is on dumpster fire, and you must go through hell to get a job. I am unemployed right now, although I think I am not applying enough effort to get a job. But it certainly not how things should work. Before AI and internet, it was indeed easier to get a job. There are a lot of junior developers in any field. And nobody really needs that much junior developers. If you are not exceptionally good or lucky, it’s going to be really rough.

Had you not changed your mind yet? Good, let us proceed then. Computer science is incredibly vast field that has a lot of stuff going on. There are computer systems, algorithms, data structures, operating systems, databases, programming languages, and much more fields of research and discoveries. I spent some time onto theory, of course, but I am not computer scientist. I am not doing research. I am developer, and I create stuff in code. If you wish to become computer scientist, it’s better to go at the university and get a proper education. If you are in USA and don’t have a lot of money, though, I would not advise you to go into college. It costs ridiculous amount of money, and a lot of information they teach is available online. How deep should you study theory to become a developer? I think that you have to find a good balance. You have to understand enough theory to grasp what is going on, but I don’t think that you must study it at university level (unless you intend on working in big tech, and even so, there are still resources like leetcode for that). Why not? It takes a tremendous amount of time and effort, and is practically useless unless you want to impress someone with your level of knowledge or intend to understand really deeply concepts you are applying. Great example of this is time complexity proof of algorithm. It uses quite a bit of advanced mathematics in attempt to show you that time complexity estimate is indeed correct. It is useful if you are genuinely curious about algorithms, and/or intend on doing some sort of research, but it is not really of much use in practice. Do not get me wrong, you must know basics about time complexity to understand that three nested loops is probably a bad idea. But I don’t think that you must know that at level of university graduate, unless you really want to. Unless you have exceptional interest and determination to study all that, general advice would be to learn the basics you really need and move on. It does not sound great, sure, but I assume that your time is limited, so you have to manage it efficiently. Otherwise, your study can take years.

What to study? Well, first thing would be an introduction into development. If you are an absolute neophyte, start with something simple. One resource I could advise here would be a course from the Khan Academy. It uses Python to teach introduction into Computer Science. Unfortunately, I can not assert quality of this course, since I don’t have enough time, but Khan Academy is generally a good place to learn.

After that, you might want to try something a little bit more advanced. Great place I would certainly recommend would be a Harvard Computer Science Introductory Course. It’s called CS50 and covers a fairly wide range of computer science on the basic level. It teaches you multiple concepts, ideas and languages. Course materials are good, and your answers can be checked automatically. Also, check out additional CS50 courses. There are courses on Python, SQL, Web development, Mobile development, Game development, AI, and Cybersecurity.

The next place to go would be a course on Computer Science theory from Khan Academy. It covers more theoretical aspects of computer science. Also, if you are curious about algorithms, you can check out book called “Algorithms Unlocked” by Thomas Cormen. Finally, if you want to dig even deeper, there is a collection of resources to study computer science deeply on your own.

That more or less covers the bare basics of theory. Something might be omitted or not included. Many other resources are mentioned in the collection above. For example, the book called “Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs” teaches you about Lisp, language which is not widely used today. But it teaches important concepts about development, like recursion, lists, trees, expression evaluation, time complexity, sequence processing and much more. I am not sure that you have to read the whole book, but first two-three chapters would be nice to study. Pretty much the same applies to other books in this collection. Figure out what you would like to know, and what could be useful. You can often read the role and purpose of material in the book. For example, in “Computer Systems: A Programmer’s Perspective”, there is a chapter about processor architecture. It shows you details on how processors work and optimize their workflow. If you are curious about processors, you might read it, but I am not sure if this is the most important material that you must know as a developer. There will be a lot of people that would arrogantly tell you what you must know, because it brings an immense theoretical value and uncovers mysteries about how things work deeply. If you are curious and want to know that, sure, go ahead and study. But if that’s not the case, then find the most important things that you need to know, and after that, move on. Remember, you are not aspiring scientist or researcher, you are developer, and this is much more practical domain to be in.
