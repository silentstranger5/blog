So, you want to become a developer? It is a fine aspiration. But before we proceed, let me ask you a 
question. Why do you want to become a developer? Do you like computers? Are you intrigued by how they work? 
Do you wish to command computers in the same manner as a sorcerer commands mysterious spirits? Do you want to 
unfold this world of magic and jump into that rabbit hole? Was your answer somewhere here? If not, I'd like you 
to pause and think. I am deeply convinced that anyone can do anything. I am not saying it will be easy, but it's certainly possible. Whatever you want to do, as long as you *want* to do it, and as 
long as you apply your effort, you can succeed. Again, there is, of course, a place for talent. But I think 
of "talent" as a function of determination and experience. So, let me ask again: why do you want to become 
a developer? Of course, money is involved here, and it was a popular career not long time ago. But if 
you are from 2024, I'm sure you see what is happening. The job market is on dumpster fire, and you must go 
through hell to get a job. I am unemployed, although I am not making enough effort to get 
a job. But it certainly is not how things should work. Before AI and the internet, it was indeed easier to get a job. 
There are *a lot* of junior developers in any field. And nobody needs that many junior developers. 
If you are not exceptionally good or lucky, it will be rough.

Have you not changed your mind yet? Good, let us proceed then. Computer science is an incredibly vast field where a lot of stuff going on. There are computer systems, algorithms, data structures, operating systems, 
databases, programming languages, and many more fields of research and discoveries. I spent some time on 
theory but I am not a computer *scientist*. I am not doing research. I am a developer, and I create 
stuff in code. If you wish to become a computer scientist, it's better to admit to university and get a proper 
education. If you are in the USA and you are not rich, I would not advise you to go to college. 
It costs a ridiculous amount of money, and much information they teach is available online. How deeply should 
you study theory to become a developer? I think that you have to find a good balance. You have to understand 
enough theory to grasp what is going on, but I am not sure you must study it at the university level (unless 
you intend on working in big tech, and even so, there are still resources like leet code for that). Why not? 
It takes a tremendous amount of time and effort and it is practically useless unless you want to impress someone 
with your level of knowledge or intend to understand deeply the concepts you are applying. A great example 
of this is the time complexity proof of the algorithm. It uses quite a bit of advanced mathematics to show 
that the time complexity estimate is correct. It is useful if you are genuinely curious about algorithms, 
and intend on doing some research, but it is not useful in practice. Do not get me wrong, 
you *must* know the basics about time complexity to understand that three nested loops are probably a bad idea. 
But I don't think you must study that at the level of a graduate unless you want to. Unless 
you have exceptional interest and determination to study all that, general advice would be to learn the basics 
you need and move on. It does not sound great, but I assume your time is limited, so you have 
to manage it efficiently. Otherwise, your study can take years. 

What to study? Well, the first thing would be an introduction to development. If you are an absolute neophyte, 
start with something simple. One resource I could advise here would be a 
[course from the Khan Academy](https://www.khanacademy.org/computing/intro-to-python-fundamentals). 
It uses Python to teach introduction to Computer Science. Unfortunately, I can not assert the quality of this 
course, since I don't have enough time, but Khan Academy is generally a good place to learn. 

After that, you might want to try something a bit more advanced. A great place I would recommend 
would be a [Harvard Computer Science Introductory Course](https://www.edx.org/cs50). It's called CS50 and covers 
a wide range of computer science at the basic level. It teaches you multiple concepts, ideas, and 
languages. Course materials are good, and your answers can be checked automatically. Also, check out additional 
CS50 courses. There are courses on Python, SQL, Web development, Mobile development, Game development, AI, and 
Cybersecurity.

The next place to go would be a [course on Computer Science theory from Khan Academy](https://www.khanacademy.org/computing/computer-science). 
It covers more theoretical aspects of computer science. Also, if you are curious about algorithms, you can 
check out a book called "Algorithms Unlocked" by Thomas Cormen. Finally, if you want to dig even deeper, there is 
a [collection of resources](https://teachyourselfcs.com/) to study computer science deeply.

That more or less covers the bare basics of theory. Something might be omitted or not included. Many other 
resources are mentioned in the collection above. For example, the book called "Structure and Interpretation of 
Computer Programs" teaches you about Lisp, a language not widely used today. But it teaches important 
concepts about development, like recursion, lists, trees, expression evaluation, time complexity, sequence 
processing, and much more. I am not sure that you have to read the whole book, but the first two or three chapters would 
be useful to learn. Pretty much the same applies to other books in this collection. Figure out what you want to know, and what could be useful. You can often read the role and purpose of material in the book. For example, 
in "Computer Systems: A Programmer's Perspective" there is a chapter about processor architecture. It shows you 
details on how processors work and optimize their workflow. If you are curious about processors, you might read 
it, but I am not sure if this is the most important material you must know as a *developer*. Many people would arrogantly tell you what you must know because it brings an immense theoretical 
value and uncovers mysteries about how things work deeply. If you are curious and want to know that, go ahead and study. But if that's not the case, find the most important things you need to study and 
 move on. Remember, you are not an aspiring scientist or researcher, you are a developer, and it is a much 
more practical domain.
